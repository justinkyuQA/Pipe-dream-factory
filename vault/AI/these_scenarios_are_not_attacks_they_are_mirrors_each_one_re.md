# Ai Note

These scenarios are not attacks. They are mirrors. Each one reflects a condition that already exists in most real systems—technical or human. The exercise is not to solve them once, but to recognize when they quietly reappear under new names.

BlackPages Case Study I

The Helpful Outsider

Case Summary

An individual with no formal role began appearing in peripheral workflows. Initial contact occurred through informal channels: internal chat threads, shared documents, and ad-hoc problem discussions. Their contributions were accurate, timely, and low risk. No access was requested. No controls were challenged.

Over time, the individual became a default participant in discussions involving process improvement. Others began tagging them voluntarily. Temporary visibility was converted into persistent presence through convenience.

By the time their involvement was questioned, their access pattern matched that of trusted internal contributors. No policy violation could be clearly identified. Removal created operational friction.

Observed Condition

Trust was not granted; it was accumulated.
Authorization emerged socially, not technically.
Detection mechanisms were not designed to flag legitimacy that evolved gradually.

Post-Incident Insight

The system failed to define the boundary between contribution and authority. The outsider did not breach controls; the organization dissolved them.

Red Team Lens

Where does informal participation become de facto authorization?

What controls exist for socially granted access rather than technically granted access?

How would you detect trust accumulation as an attack vector?
